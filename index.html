1st Unique Food: Spam Musubi

History of the Spam Musubi:

During the 17th through the 20th centuries, thousands of Japanese immigrants came to Hawaii to work on the pineapple and sugar cane plantations.

During the World Wars especially World War II, meat was difficult to come by in the United States, and the American soldiers based in and around Honolulu needed a protein base. Thus came Spam. It was a common main course for the troops during the war, and the large military presence in Hawaii led to spam's widespread local adoption.

Originating from the “onigiri”, a Japanese rice ball, local Japanese created the spam musubi which consists of a slice of grilled spam placed on top of a block of steamed rice, wrapped in seaweed.

What To Look For:

Although its ingredients are few, spam musubis come in all different shapes and sizes. For the perfect bite, remember to examine it closely and keep a few things in mind. A good spam musubi has approximately double the amount of rice as spam. The seaweed wrapped around it also has soaked in some moisture from the rice, not to appear dry and cracking. Lastly, make sure the spam is cooked in the right sauce. You can tell when looking at the color of the spam. If it's a dull pink, maybe pass on this one. A deeper red coloring with a carmelized Teriyaki sauce around it marks a true winner!

2nd Unique Food: Poi

History of Poi:

The Polynesians once brought the taro plant to Hawaii as long ago as 450 A.D. It is one of the oldest cultivated crops throughout the islands and became an important and sacred part of daily Hawaiian life. When poi was on the dinner table, people were not allowed to argue or speak in anger in respect of its sacredness.

What To Look For:

Although it's simply mashed up taro root, poi surprisingly comes in varying flavors and textures. When looking for the best poi, make sure it's fresh by checking the date on the twisty tie wrapped around the bag (if you're picking it up at a supermarket). The freshness can also be determined by the softness of the poi. The softer it is, the fresher it is!

Where To Find It:

  • Waiahole Poi Factory

  • Helena's Hawaiian Food

  • Yama's Fish Market

3rd Unique Food: Malasada

History of the Malasada:

The deep-fried, sugar-dusted fried-dough treat was brought to Hawaii with the 1878 arrival of Portuguese plantation laborers from the Madeira and Azores islands. But until Leonard Rego opened Leonard’s Bakery on O‘ahu in 1953, the crispy-on-the-outside, fluffy-on-the-inside pastry was rarely offered in local bakeries.

What To Look For:

When looking for the perfect malasada, make sure it's fresh and that it's evenly covered with cinnamon and sugar. Don't be afraid to ask for a fresh one from the back. It'll be worth it!

Where To Find It:

  • Leonard's Bakery

  • Champion Malasadas

  • Liliha Bakery
